The Problem

Not All Customers Are the Same

Imagine you run an online retail store. You have thousands of customers ‚Äî some buy frequently and spend a lot, while others made one purchase months ago and haven't returned.

Treating all customers equally is a mistake. Sending the same marketing message to a loyal VIP and an inactive customer wastes resources and misses opportunities.

This project answers three key business questions:

  • Who are our most loyal customers?
  • Which customers are at risk of churning?
  • Which segments have the highest upselling potential?

üìà Finding the Right Number of Groups

A critical step in clustering is deciding how many groups to create. Too few and you miss nuances; too many and the groups become meaningless.

I used two mathematical techniques to determine the optimal number:

  • Elbow Method ‚Äî looks for the point of diminishing returns in grouping quality
  • Silhouette Analysis ‚Äî measures how well each customer fits their assigned group

Both methods pointed to K=2 as the optimal number of clusters.

üìê RFM Feature Engineering

‚ñæ

Transaction-level data was aggregated into three customer-level metrics:

‚Ä¢ Recency (R): Days since the customer's last purchase
‚Ä¢ Frequency (F): Total number of unique transactions
‚Ä¢ Monetary (M): Total revenue generated by the customer

These three numbers give a complete behavioral snapshot of each customer.

üîÑ Data Transformation

‚ñæ

K-Means clustering works best with normally distributed, equally-scaled features:

‚Ä¢ Log Transformation: Applied log1p() to handle highly right-skewed distributions in all three RFM metrics
‚Ä¢ Standardization: Used StandardScaler so all features exist on the same scale (Mean=0, SD=1)

üß† K-Means Clustering & PCA

‚ñæ

The K-Means algorithm was applied with the Elbow Method and Silhouette Analysis to determine the optimal K=2.

PCA (Principal Component Analysis) was used to reduce 3 dimensions to 2 for visualization, revealing clear separation between the clusters.
