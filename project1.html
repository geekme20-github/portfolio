The Problem

Why Predicting Churn Matters

Imagine running a telecom company with thousands of customers. Every month, some cancel their service ‚Äî that's churn. Each lost customer costs the company real money in missed revenue and the expense of acquiring replacements.

The question is: Can we predict which customers are about to leave, before they actually do?

If we can identify at-risk customers early, the company can offer targeted incentives ‚Äî a discount, a better plan, or personalized support ‚Äî to convince them to stay. This is far cheaper than finding new customers.

The Solution

How It Works

I built three machine learning models and deployed the best one as an interactive dashboard where you input customer details and get an instant churn risk score.

üß† The Models

Three different algorithms were trained and compared to find the best predictor:

üìä Key Insights

The model reveals which factors most strongly predict whether a customer will leave:

  • Contract type is the #1 predictor ‚Äî month-to-month customers are at highest risk
  • Internet service type ‚Äî fiber optic customers churn more than DSL customers
  • Tenure ‚Äî newer customers are more likely to leave than long-term ones
  • Payment method ‚Äî electronic check users have higher churn rates

üîç Data Exploration & Cleaning

‚ñæ

The dataset contains 7,043 telecom customers with 21 features covering demographics, account information, and services subscribed. Key cleaning steps included:

‚Ä¢ Converting TotalCharges from string to numeric and handling missing values
‚Ä¢ Engineering new features like AvgMonthlySpend and NumServices
‚Ä¢ One-hot encoding categorical variables (Contract, Internet Service, Payment Method)

‚öôÔ∏è Feature Engineering & Scaling

‚ñæ

19 features were used after encoding. Numerical features (tenure, MonthlyCharges, TotalCharges, NumServices, AvgMonthlySpend) were standardized using StandardScaler to ensure equal weighting across all models.

The scaler and feature names were saved as .joblib artifacts for production deployment.

ü§ñ Model Training & Comparison

‚ñæ

Three models were trained: Logistic Regression (baseline), Random Forest, and XGBoost. Each was evaluated using accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC.

XGBoost emerged as the best performer, with particularly strong recall on the churn class ‚Äî crucial since missing a churner is more costly than a false alarm.
