The Problem

Should We Move the Gate?

Cookie Cats is a popular mobile puzzle game. Like many games, it has "gates" ‚Äî points where players must wait or make a purchase before continuing. These gates force a break in gameplay.

The game's designers wondered: What happens if we move the first gate from Level 30 to Level 40? Would players enjoy more uninterrupted play, or would the longer session lead to burnout?

To answer this scientifically, they ran an A/B test ‚Äî randomly assigning 90,189 new players to either the original (gate at 30) or the new version (gate at 40) and measuring what happened.

The Verdict

Keep the Gate at Level 30

The data tells a clear story: moving the gate later hurts player retention. The original design is better.

üîç Why Does This Happen?

The phenomenon is called "hedonic adaptation" ‚Äî the psychological principle that enjoyment decreases with prolonged, uninterrupted exposure.

By forcing a break at Level 30, the game actually prevents player burnout. Players return refreshed and more likely to keep playing long-term.

Moving the gate to Level 40 gives players more playtime before their first break, but paradoxically reduces their motivation to return.

üí° Business Recommendation

Do not move the gate to Level 40. The current placement at Level 30 is optimal for long-term player retention and engagement.

üßπ Data Cleaning & Outlier Detection

‚ñæ

An extreme outlier was identified in the gate_30 group (49,854 game rounds) ‚Äî far beyond normal play. This single point threatened to bias the mean and was removed.

The game rounds distribution was highly right-skewed (Skewness: ~185), meaning most players play very few rounds while a small number play thousands. This justified using non-parametric statistical tests.

üî¨ Bootstrapping Methodology

‚ñæ

Bootstrapping is a powerful statistical technique that works by repeatedly resampling the data to estimate the uncertainty in our measurements:

1. Randomly sample (with replacement) from each group 1,000 times
2. Calculate the retention rate for each resample
3. Compare the distributions of retention rates between groups
4. Compute the probability that one group outperforms the other

This approach is robust and makes minimal assumptions about the underlying data distribution.

üìä Mann-Whitney U Test

‚ñæ

The Mann-Whitney U test was used for engagement analysis because the game rounds data is extremely non-normal. The test returned a p-value of 0.0509, suggesting a marginal but notable difference in distribution, favoring the earlier gate.

While not statistically significant at the traditional Œ±=0.05 level, combined with the strong retention results, this further supports keeping the gate at Level 30.
